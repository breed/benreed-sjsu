# the 10 golden rules for success as a CMPE student

{:start="0"}
0. **ask questions!**
   at SJSU you have professors who are experts in their domain.
   they also have a desire to share their knowledge with you.
   initially, that will be through lectures, but you can get lectures on youtube that are much more entertaining.
   unlike youtube, you have the expert right in front of you that you can ask when you don't understand.
   so, if you don't understand, **ask the question**.
   you may be scared that the question is stupid, but there will probably be many students in the class who will be happy that you asked.
1. **be in class!**
   don't skip class!
   we don't take attendance at SJSU, so it's easy to skip class, but you will miss out on a big part of learning.
   a good class will provide you with reading material, assignments, and lecture.
   lectures can emphasize and review key concepts in a different format from the readings --- they give you a chance to see the subject in a different way.
   prepare for lectures by doing the reading before class.
   this will help you understand the lecture and know your gaps in understanding so that you can ask questions.
   **actually be in the class** by turning off discord and engaging with the lecture.
   working on assignments for other classes during class is counterproductive.
2. **read the textbook!**
   to be a good engineer, you need to be able to read technical documents.
   textbooks are great technical reads to start with.
   AI summaries of readings, notes from others, and slides are no substitute for reading the text.
   skipping the readings will reduce the depth of your understanding and compromise the development of an important skill that you will need to excel in your career.
3. **start the assignments on the day you get them!**
   assignments get harder the closer you get to the deadline --- try it; you'll see.
   usually content needed for the assignment will be covered in class.
   if you are working on the assignment, while learning the subject you will:

   - apply the concepts as you learn them --- reinforcing the learning.
   - find gaps in your understanding when you get stuck --- you can ask questions in class to fill those gaps.
   - have time to make mistakes; figure out what the problem is; get it working --- one of the best learning cycles there is.

   a great workflow that a professor gave me in my abstract algebra class:

   1. start the assignment immediately and work on it until you are stuck
   2. do something else --- play video games, work on another class, go for a run, go to sleep
   3. work on the assignment again. you might finish. your subconscious will work on the problem and you will wake up with some insights --- remember this was a math prof, not a psychologist, so this might not be the best explanation for why it works :)
   4. if you don't make progress go to office hours!
4. **be on campus!**
   if you are arriving on campus just before your class and jetting out right after, you are missing out on much of the value of your university experience.
   you'll also often arrive late and frazzled to class.
   get to class an hour before your first class and plan on staying at least one hour later.
   you are supposed to spend at a minimum 2 hours for every hour in class according to SJSU policy.
   that's the minimum.
   find a study group and study together.
   make sure you bring your ideas.
   this is the beginning of your professional network.
   connect on linkedin.
   join clubs --- you'll learn a lot about working with others.
5. **get a laptop for school (not for games)!**
   you will use your laptop A LOT!
   you need:

   - long battery life (at least 10 hours)
   - USB-C charging --- everyone will have an adapter you can borrow if needed
   - at least a 15" screen with 1080 resolution. if you want to get serious, there are great 15.6" external panels that make light second screens
   - light weight --- you'll carry this thing with you everywhere
   - 16G memory at least --- some apps are going to use a lot of ram, especially if you are using a VM
   - 500G (preferably 1T) of storage. you're going to install a lot of stuff

   you have probably realized that the above eliminates most gaming laptops --- i love gaming, but use a desktop at home.
   i regularly check the laptops at costco and best buy.
   you can regularly find laptops matching the above for close to $500.
6. **hone your skills!**
   if you only are working on becoming a good computer engineer or software engineer during class, you are not going to graduate with the skills you need --- especially now with AI.
   the assignments and projects you do in class are not enough.
   they help you learn, but to really hone your skills you need to practice outside of class.

   come up with a regimen.
   for me this means:

   - writing code every day --- some days it's just a simple script.
   - having personal projects. you can look at my github for examples of the public ones i work on. there are more private ones.
   - reading books on new subjects. yeah, i'm finally starting to learn the math behind llms.
   - learning new skills --- i'm finally learning FPGAs this summer!

   as a student you have the opportunity to get involved with spartan racing, robotics club, competitive programming team, ML club, etc.
   you will learn new things and meet new people.

7. **AI is not for learning!**
   claude is one of my best friends.
   we've done a lot together, but i don't learn from claude.
   sometimes i get pointers to resources --- papers, books, talks --- to learn new things.
   when i use AI, i trade off learning for productivity.
   there have been studies that show that claude makes us stupider --- without the struggle our brain marks knowledge as unneeded and knows it can rely on a tool for the knowledge.

   avoid the temptation to use AI to do homework, and especially assignments.
   early in my career i used to think, "if i can see the code i will know the solution", but without some foundational knowledge --- that usually comes with my figuring out the solution --- the solution doesn't mean much to me.

8. **stay healthy!**
   if you get sick, you miss class and you get behind on assignments.
   mental health issues or severe illness in the family can take you away from school.
   SJSU recognizes the importance of taking care of yourself and provides ways to do late drops of classes for medical reasons --- take advantage of them if you need to.
   a kind instructor will push deadlines for you, but the end of semester cannot be pushed.
   i would love to be able to say "wow you passed through something terrible. i'll give you the knowledge without having to struggle."
   --- i really would.
   the best we can do is allow you to pass without learning the material, but that is detrimental to you and your career.
   you can convince some instructors to do it --- i do not; it would not be doing you a favor.
   if you need to, repeat the class; master the material.
   
   it's a lot of work to catch up on class after you get sick --- to pass the class you have to have a level of mastery of the material.
   so, avoid getting sick.
   "but someone gave me a virus" you say; "it's not my fault" you say; "i have kids!" --- been there; done that!
   that is all true, but there are things we can do to stay healthy: get sleep, find ways to relieve stress, eat healthy, wash your hands, remove habits like rubbing eyes and touching face, and get vaccinated.
   it's not going to guarantee that you don't get sick, but it will lower the probability.
   when you do get sick, take care of yourself to recover quickly, so that you can put in the work to catch up.
   **get a good night's sleep before exams** --- students make some silly mistakes when they are tired.

9. **don't cheat!**
    when you cheat, you are wasting your time and money!
    10 years ago, a CMPE and SE degree guaranteed you a job.
    plenty of students who weren't prepared obtained good positions because they had a degree.
    nowadays, what you know is more important than a degree.
    cheating undermines your preparation.
    the biggest reason i've heard for cheating is "i ran out of time", so follow the *start the assignment when you get it* rule.

10. **prepare for senior project early!**
    yeah i know, this is 11, but hey, off-by-one errors are our thing!
    engineering degrees give you a fantastic opportunity to do a senior project that highlights your skills.
    if done correctly, you will have something fantastic to discuss in your job interviews.
    to make that happen you will need a good group, a good topic, and a good advisor.
    find your group at the start of your junior year and get an idea of a topic.
    find your advisor and lock in your topic at the beginning of your 2nd semester of your junior year.
    good advisors will work with you to come up with a topic that you and the advisor are interested in and your advisor has the background to help you on your project.
